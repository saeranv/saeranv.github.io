Notes by Saeran Vasanthakumar --- 21/02/15 - Solving Heat Conduction Part I For 1D steady state heat conduction, a simple way to solve for intermediate temperatures given a material's boundary conditions is: \(y_{int} = H \cdot X_{ext} \) \(\begin{bmatrix} T_{int} \\ q_{int} \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & R_1 d_1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} T_{ext} \\ q_{ext} \\ \end{bmatrix} \) Where: \( \begin{align} &R_i = \text{ Resistivity at the } i_{th} \text{ layer in } \frac{mK}{W} \\ &d = \text{ depth in } m \\ &T = \text{ Temperature in } K \\ &q = \text{ Heat flux in } \frac{W}{m2} \\ \end{align} \) Applying the matrix transformation results in the following formula: \( \begin{align} &T_{ext} = T_{int} + q_{int} \cdot R_1 d_1 \\ &q_{ext} = q_{int} \\ \end{align} \) \( \text{Note for (1): } K + (W/m2 * (mK/W) \cdot m) = K \\ \) The initial, boundary temperature and heat flux is provided, and then subsequent state values are updated according to specific layer resistivity and depth. Note that \(q\) (a constant value in this steady-state problem) can be reformulated as the ratio of total temperature, depth and \(R\)-value: \(q_{ext} = q_{int} = q_{total} = \frac{R d}{\Delta T}\) Which illustrates familiar relationship between the ratio of total temperature, \(R\) and a specific layer: \( \begin{align} \frac{\Delta T_{i}}{\Delta T_{total}} &= \frac{R_{i} d_{i}}{R_{total} d_{total}} \end{align} \) Footnotes 2.

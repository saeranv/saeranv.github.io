Notes by Saeran Vasanthakumar --- 21/02/15 - Modeling Steady-state Heat and Temperature Matrices For steady state (no mass) simulation, you can model the state of temperature and heat flow through a material by: \(y_{int} = H \cdot X_{ext} \) \(\begin{bmatrix} T_{int} \\ q_{int} \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & R_1 d_1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} T_{ext} \\ q_{ext} \\ \end{bmatrix} \) Where: \( \begin{align} &R_i = \text{ Resistivity at the } i_{th} \text{ layer in } \frac{mK}{W} \\ &d = \text{ depth in } m \\ &T = \text{ Temperature in } K \\ &q = \text{ Heat flux in } \frac{W}{m2} \\ \end{align} \) Applying the matrix transformation results in the following formula: \( \begin{align} &T_{ext} = T_{int} + q_{int} \cdot R_1 d_1 \\ &q_{ext} = q_{int} \\ \end{align} \) \( \text{Note for (1): } K + (W/m2 * (mK/W) \cdot m) = K \\ \) The initial, boundary temperature and heat flux is provided, and then subsequent state values are updated according to specific layer resistivity and depth. Note that \(q\) (a constant value in this steady-state problem) can be reformulated as the ratio of total temperature, depth and \(R\)-value: \(q_{ext} = q_{int} = q_{total} = \frac{R d}{\Delta T}\) Which illustrates familiar relationship between the ratio of total temperature, \(R\) and a specific layer: \( \begin{align} \frac{\Delta T_{i}}{\Delta T_{total}} &= \frac{R_{i} d_{i}}{R_{total} d_{total}} \end{align} \) The matrix representation of this formula gives us an efficient way to compose multiple, parallel layers (i.e a series circuit) together to calculate simple multi-layer assemblies: \( y_{ext} = H_3 \cdot H_2 \cdot H_1 \cdot X_{int} \) References 1. Bassols, Xavier Botey. "The use of heat transfer functions for heat flow computation through multilayer walls". Masters Thesis. 2013. 2. Conduction Through The Walls.
